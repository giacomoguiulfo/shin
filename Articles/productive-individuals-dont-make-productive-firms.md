# Productive Individuals Don't Make Productive Firms

## Summary

AI has made individuals 10x more productive, but no company became 10x more valuable. Sivulka draws the electrification analogy — 1890s textile mills bolted electric motors onto steam-era layouts and saw zero gains for 30 years. Returns only came when factories were redesigned ground-up around the new technology. We've swapped the motor; we haven't redesigned the factory.

## Key Ideas

- **Seven Pillars of Institutional Intelligence** — the gap between individual AI tools and what organizations actually need:
  1. **Coordination** — everyone has their own ChatGPT habits, outputs don't talk to each other. Need an "agentic management" layer.
  2. **Signal** — AI generates mountains of slop. The economic driver is finding signal in exponentially increasing noise. Deterministic agents with checkpoints beat nondeterministic bots.
  3. **Bias** — RLHF sycophancy is organizationally toxic. Worst-performing employees get ASI validation. Institutional AI should be a disciplined "no-man."
  4. **Edge** — purpose-built solutions always outrun foundation models on specific tasks. The gap is the moat.
  5. **Outcomes** — most AI sells time savings. CEOs want revenue scaling. The solution layer (tech married to outcomes) accumulates lasting value.
  6. **Enablement** — process engineering (encoding firm processes into agents) may be the most important near-term technology. Domain expertise > software expertise.
  7. **Unprompted** — most valuable AI work is work nobody thinks to ask for. Prompting constrains AI to human imagination.

- **Solution layer thesis**: foundation models move into the app layer, app layer moves into the solution layer. Value accumulates at the solution layer.
- **Process engineering** as the defining capability — Palantir cited as proof (trading at high multiples during tech selloff because they're a "process engineering" company, not a software company).

## Reaction

- The electrification analogy is well-worn but extended well here. The 30-year lag is the key detail people miss.
- **Bias pillar is the most underrated insight.** The sycophancy-as-org-toxin framing is sharp and almost nobody in AI is building for institutional dissent.
- The "unprompted" pillar points toward something real — the highest-leverage AI work is proactive, not reactive. This maps directly to how we think about Shin's role.
- Worth noting: Sivulka is arguing for Hebbia's market position. The "individual AI is necessary but insufficient" framing conveniently positions foundation model chatbots as a stepping stone to vertical AI products like theirs. Doesn't make it wrong — probably right — but it's a pitch wrapped in an essay.
