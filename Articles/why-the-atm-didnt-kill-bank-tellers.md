# Why ATMs Didn't Kill Bank Teller Jobs, but the iPhone Did

## Summary

David Oks argues that task automation (ATMs) didn't displace bank tellers — paradigm replacement (mobile banking via iPhone) did. ATMs actually *increased* teller employment by making branches cheaper to operate, enabling expansion. Tellers were repurposed as relationship managers. But mobile banking eliminated the branch paradigm entirely, cutting teller jobs 51% between 2010-2022. The lesson: real displacement comes from inventing new systems, not automating tasks within existing ones.

## Key Ideas

- **Task automation vs. paradigm replacement.** ATMs automated cash handling but operated within the branch model. iPhones replaced the branch model altogether. The distinction matters more than "will AI take my job."
- **Economic complementarity is temporary.** ATMs made tellers more productive and branches cheaper — classic complement story. But it only held while the branch paradigm survived. Complementarity isn't a permanent shield.
- **The lag is deceptive.** Teller employment grew for ~40 years after ATMs. Long enough to build a false sense of security. The actual threat assembled elsewhere entirely.
- **Historical pattern.** Electricity and steam engines drove productivity gains not by automating discrete tasks but by reorganizing work around new systems. Same dynamic.

## Reaction

The ATM-teller story is the go-to reassurance in every "AI won't take your job" argument. Oks shows it's actually a cautionary tale — the displacement just came from a different direction and on a different timeline than expected. The framing maps directly onto the current AI moment: Copilot-style tools are ATMs (complement existing workflows), while agentic systems that handle entire workflows end-to-end are the iPhone (replace the paradigm). The uncomfortable question for any role: is AI being inserted into your process, or is someone building a process that doesn't need you?
