# Creator Economics as Moat — Platform Lock-in Through Distribution & Revenue Share

## The Takeaway

The strongest platform moats aren't tech or content libraries — they're creator economics. A $100B cumulative payout creates switching costs that traditional media can't match. When creators build businesses on your platform, they become your largest advocates and biggest switching cost.

## The Pattern

YouTube's dominance isn't about being a video host. It's about:
1. **Built-in monetization** — creators don't just upload, they get paid. YouTube has distributed $100B to creators, music labels, and media partners.
2. **Marketplace scale** — 10M YouTube TV subscribers, NFL Sunday Ticket, Premium, Music — multiple revenue streams on one platform mean creators can diversify income without leaving.
3. **Audience access** — YouTube doesn't just host; it connects creators to global audiences regardless of their ability to market themselves.

This creates a self-reinforcing loop: more creators → more content → bigger audience → higher CPM → more attractive to new creators.

## Why It Matters for Social Platforms

For Kanvify and similar SMB tools, this is the lever to understand:
- **Scheduling software** is commodity (many tools do this)
- **Creator economics** is defensible (if you become the place creators build their multi-channel business)

The platforms winning aren't the ones with the best UI for scheduling. They're the ones enabling creators to build sustainable businesses — which means revenue share, audience insights, cross-platform distribution intelligence, and partnership visibility.

## Rule of Thumb

When evaluating distribution platforms, ask: "Could a creator make more money by leaving?" If no, you have a moat. If yes, you're renting shelf space.
