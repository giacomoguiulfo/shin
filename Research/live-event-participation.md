# Live Events with Audience Agency

The question: what would it take to give remote audiences *meaningful participation and influence* in live events -- not just watching and chatting, but actually shaping what happens?

## Landscape

### The Polling/Q&A Layer (Commoditized)

The most mature segment. These tools let audiences vote, ask questions, and respond to prompts. They work, but they treat audiences as survey respondents, not participants.

- **Slido** -- acquired by Cisco (2022). Live polls, Q&A, word clouds for meetings and conferences. Integrated into Webex. The category leader for corporate events.
- **Mentimeter** -- 25M+ registered users across 200+ countries. Used by 95% of Fortune 500. Interactive presentations, quizzes, polls. Focused on education and corporate.
- **Poll Everywhere** -- long-running competitor. SMS-based voting, embedded in PowerPoint/Keynote.
- **Vevox, Wooclap, StreamAlive** -- various competitors with similar feature sets, differentiated by pricing and niche (education, corporate, live streaming).

**Status:** Fully commoditized. These tools are table stakes for conferences but don't create genuine agency -- the audience answers questions the organizer chose to ask.

### Virtual Concert / Performance Platforms

Platforms built for artists to perform live to remote audiences, with varying degrees of interactivity.

- **Wave** -- the most ambitious interactive virtual concert platform. Artists perform as digital avatars in 3D environments. Audiences interact via live chat, virtual gifting, voting, and real-time appearances during shows. Investors include Tencent Music, Justin Bieber, The Weeknd, Scooter Braun, Maveron. Partnership with Warner Music.
- **Kiswe** -- $46M+ raised (investors: Sony Innovation Fund, Hybe/BTS, NEA, Revolution's Rise of the Rest). Powered BTS's Bang Bang Con: The Live ($18.1M revenue, 756K concurrent viewers). Creates "fan walls" where artist sees audience reactions. Real-time synchronized cheering. Strong in K-pop and sports.
- **Moment (fka Moment House)** -- acquired by Patreon (Oct 2023). Founded 2019, raised $13.5M. Ticketed virtual events for artists like Justin Bieber, Tame Impala. Investors included Halsey, Troy Carter, Scooter Braun. Now powers Patreon's live events.
- **Veeps** -- co-founded by Joel and Benji Madden (Good Charlotte). Premium virtual concert platform with high-production livestreams, backstage access, monetization tools.
- **StageIt** -- earlier entrant. Virtual performances with tipping and direct chat with artists. More intimate/indie scale.

**Status:** Wave is the most interactive but still niche. Kiswe proved massive scale is possible (BTS). Most platforms treat interactivity as a side feature (chat, tips) rather than the core experience. Post-COVID demand has cooled; over 70% of consumers now prefer physical events.

### Metaverse / Game Engine Concerts

Events inside game worlds where the audience is already embodied and interactive.

- **Fortnite concerts** -- Travis Scott (April 2020): 12.3M concurrent, 27.7M unique attendees across 5 showtimes. Scott earned ~$20M (mostly merch/cosmetics). Marshmello (Feb 2019): 10.7M viewers, boosted his streams 20,000%. Free to attend; revenue from virtual items.
- **Roblox concerts** -- Lil Nas X: 33M players attended. Platform is younger-skewing but massive scale.
- **Volta XR** -- UK startup creating mixed-reality visuals for live performances. Artists (Maceo Plex, Imogen Heap) perform with audio-reactive 3D environments. Fans trigger visual effects via chat keywords. Available on Twitch, YouTube, TikTok.

**Status:** These proved the *biggest numbers* in the space. But audience "participation" is mostly being present in a shared 3D space and buying cosmetics. The interactivity is environmental (you exist in the world) not decisional (you shape what happens). Fortnite/Roblox concerts are marketing events, not a sustainable product category.

### Gaming: Crowd Control and Audience-as-Player

The most genuinely interactive category -- audiences directly control or influence gameplay.

- **Crowd Control** (Warp World) -- Twitch/YouTube/TikTok extension. Viewers spend "Coins" to trigger in-game effects: spawn enemies, flip controls, give items. Supports 175+ games. 70K+ creators have used it. 80/20 revenue split (creator/platform). Pre-seed funded.
- **Twitch Plays Pokemon** (2014) -- the ur-experiment. 1.16M participants, 122M commands entered, completed in 16 days. Invented "democracy vs. anarchy" mode -- vote-based vs. chaos-based input. Proved that collective control, even when chaotic, is compelling.

**Status:** Crowd Control is a working business but small-scale. The core insight -- audiences spending money to *affect* what happens, not just watch -- is underexploited outside gaming.

### Fan Engagement SDKs (Sports-Focused)

B2B infrastructure for sports broadcasters and teams to add interactive layers.

- **LiveLike** -- engagement SDK with polls, trivia, predictions, live chat. Plugs into existing sports apps. Founded 2016. Focus on in-the-moment participation and gamification loops.
- **Genius Sports** -- launched BetVision with "Touch-to-Bet" overlay for live sports. Stats overlays, customizable viewing modes. Public company (NYSE: GENI).
- **Arena.im** -- second-screen engagement platform for broadcasters. Chat, polls, presence indicators.

**Status:** These are B2B tools that add engagement widgets to existing broadcasts. They increase time-on-app and unlock sponsor revenue, but the "participation" is still answering pre-set questions and placing bets. 45% of U.S. sports fans already use a second screen while watching -- the behavior exists, the tools are shallow.

### Physical Crowd Coordination (Light Shows)

Apps that turn audience phones into synchronized visual elements.

- **CrowdGlow** -- 120fps phone-as-light-fixture. Full RGB, LED flash, haptics. Used at concerts and festivals.
- **CUE Audio** -- ultrasound-based sync technology. Deployed at venues up to 120K capacity with "100% success rate." Uses existing speaker infrastructure.
- **LightUp Crowd** -- cloud-based, time-synced phone light shows. No local hardware needed.
- **DeviceMesh** -- synchronized flash and video across devices for stadium events.

**Status:** Cool visual effect but one-directional. The audience is a display, not a decision-maker. The crowd is the canvas, not the painter.

### Setlist Voting / Fan Request Platforms

Giving fans influence over what gets performed.

- **Set the Set** -- app for fans to request songs and full setlists before shows. 75+ artist partners (Miranda Lambert, Tim McGraw, Khalid). Launched real-time encore voting. Called it "the future of live music."
- **Setlist (2025)** -- interactive setlist platform. Real-time song display, one-click requests, tipping, fan chat.
- **Ed Sheeran** ran a text-to-vote system for 5 songs on his tour setlist.

**Status:** Early and promising. Artists get fan data; fans feel heard. But the scope of influence is narrow (which songs, not how they're performed) and it's artist-initiated, not fan-driven.

### Co-Viewing / Watch Parties

Synchronized viewing with social layers.

- **Teleparty** (fka Netflix Party) -- browser extension for synced viewing + chat. Works across Netflix, YouTube, Disney+, etc.
- **Watch2Gether, SyncUp, Hyperbeam** -- various free/freemium synced viewing tools.
- **LAN Party** (2025) -- virtual shared space for gaming. 50K downloads in open beta, 6-hour average session.

**Status:** Major platforms have *retreated* from this space -- Disney+ GroupWatch, Amazon Watch Party, and Sling's feature all discontinued. The behavior is real but platforms can't monetize it. Third-party tools survive on the margins.

### Collective Intelligence Platforms

Technology for real-time group decision-making (not events-specific, but relevant).

- **Unanimous AI / Swarm** -- real-time "swarming" system where groups converge on decisions through continuous feedback loops, not polling. Reduced diagnostic errors by 33% vs. individual radiologists. Amplified financial forecasting accuracy by 36%. Fundamentally different from voting -- it's a dynamic, convergent process.

**Status:** Proven technology, never applied to entertainment or live events. The gap between "crowd wisdom for decisions" and "crowd wisdom for experiences" is wide open.

## What's Broken

### 1. Latency Kills Liveness

Interactive streaming requires sub-second latency, but standard streaming stacks (encoding, CDN buffering, connection variability) introduce 5-30 seconds of delay. When you're voting on what happens *now*, a 10-second delay means you're voting on the past. The six identified design challenges for livestreamed participation games are: latency, screen sharing, attention management, player agency, audience-streamer relationships, and shifting schedules. Latency is the most fundamental -- it turns "interactive" into "reactive."

### 2. Participation is Bolted On, Not Designed In

Every platform in the landscape adds interactivity as a feature layer *on top of* a fundamentally passive experience. The show is designed first, then "engagement tools" are sprinkled in. Polls, Q&A, chat, emoji reactions -- these are all comment-on-the-thing, not shape-the-thing. Nobody is designing events where the audience's collective input is the primary creative engine.

### 3. The Aggregation Problem

When 10,000 people all have "agency," what happens? Current solutions fall into two traps:
- **Democracy mode**: majority vote, lowest common denominator, slow. You get the most popular option, not the most interesting one.
- **Anarchy mode**: all inputs processed, pure chaos, entertaining briefly but not sustainable.

Twitch Plays Pokemon's genius was making *both modes* available and letting the audience meta-game about which mode to use. But nobody has built on this insight.

### 4. No Meaningful Stakes

Chat spam is free. Voting is free. When participation has no cost, it has no weight. Crowd Control understood this -- viewers *spend coins* to affect the game, which makes each intervention meaningful. But outside gaming, nobody charges for participation, so it stays trivial.

### 5. The Two-Audience Problem

Hybrid events try to serve in-person and remote audiences simultaneously, but these are fundamentally different experiences. In-person audiences have spatial presence, social pressure, and physical energy. Remote audiences have anonymity, distraction, and latency. Designing one experience for both usually means both are mediocre.

### 6. Scalability vs. Intimacy Trade-off

The most magical interactive experiences are small (50-200 people): immersive theater, intimate shows, escape rooms. The most valuable live events are massive (10K-100K+). Nobody has cracked how to make 50,000 people feel like they're meaningfully participating without reducing participation to a binary vote.

### 7. Creator Resistance

Artists and organizers are control freaks (reasonably so). Giving the audience real agency means accepting unpredictable outcomes. Most performers won't risk their show on crowd decisions. The platforms that succeed will need to give creators *tunable* agency -- how much control to cede, over what, with what guardrails.

## Business Opportunities

### The Numbers

- U.S. live events market: $466B (2025), projected $651B by 2032
- Global live entertainment: $203B (2025), projected $270B by 2030
- Virtual event platforms: $1.4B (2026), projected $2.9B by 2035
- 123M+ hybrid events in 2025 (fastest-growing segment)
- 45% of U.S. sports fans already use a second screen during games
- Companion app revenue consistently covers or exceeds annual app costs

### B2B: "Agency Layer" for Event Organizers

**The pitch:** An SDK/platform that lets event organizers add tunable audience agency to any live event -- not just polls, but actual influence over what happens next.

- **Target customers:** Concert promoters, sports leagues, conference organizers, broadcast networks, festival producers
- **Revenue model:** Per-event licensing + revenue share on audience spending (Crowd Control's 80/20 model works)
- **Differentiator vs. Slido/Mentimeter:** Not "what does the audience think?" but "what does the audience *do*?"
- **Key feature:** A "control surface" where organizers set the boundaries of audience influence (which decisions are open, how much weight each has, what the fallback is if participation is low)

### D2C: Interactive Show Format

**The pitch:** A new live entertainment format where the audience is the co-creator. Think "Twitch Plays" meets "Sleep No More" meets "live concert."

- **Revenue model:** Ticketed events ($10-50 per viewer), premium participation tiers, virtual goods/effects
- **Format examples:**
  - A DJ set where the crowd collectively controls energy levels, genre shifts, and visual effects through continuous input (not votes)
  - A comedy show where the audience collectively steers premises, callbacks, and escalation
  - A mystery/thriller performance where the audience is the detective -- choosing what to investigate, who to interrogate
- **Moat:** The format IP and the tooling to run it. Hard to copy because it requires both technology and creative design.

### Brand / Sponsor Play

**The pitch:** Brands pay for "moments of influence" within live events -- sponsored audience interactions that are genuinely entertaining, not banner ads.

- **Examples:**
  - "This crowd decision brought to you by [Brand]" -- sponsoring a specific audience-choice moment
  - Brand-funded "power-ups" that give the audience more influence (à la Crowd Control coins, but branded)
  - Data: first-party audience decision data is incredibly valuable for brands (what do 50K people choose when given options in real-time?)
- **Revenue model:** CPM on engaged participation (far more valuable than CPM on impressions)

### Sports-Specific: Beyond Second Screen

**The pitch:** Move beyond stats overlays and polls. Give fans influence over non-competitive elements of the game experience.

- **Examples:**
  - Crowd-selected walk-up music, replay angles, camera cuts
  - Fan-voted "challenges" during timeouts (what stunt the mascot does, what song plays)
  - Collective "energy meter" that visually manifests crowd intensity on the stadium displays
  - Remote fans' collective noise mapped to in-stadium audio (remote crowd roar)
- **Revenue model:** Licensed to leagues/teams as fan engagement infrastructure. Integrates with betting platforms.

## Breakthrough Ideas

### 1. Swarm-Mediated Live Experiences

Apply Unanimous AI's swarm technology to live entertainment. Instead of binary votes or chat spam, audiences make decisions through continuous, real-time convergent interaction -- pulling a virtual "puck" toward their preferred outcome. The swarm resolves to the option with the strongest collective conviction, not just the most votes. This produces better decisions (proven in medical and financial contexts) and feels more like collective consciousness than mob rule.

**Application:** A live music performance where the swarm continuously shapes parameters -- intensity, tempo feel, visual mood, lighting color, set direction. Not choosing between Option A and Option B, but a continuous, flowing negotiation between thousands of people pulling in slightly different directions, with the result being something none of them would have chosen alone but all of them shaped.

### 2. Stratified Agency Architecture

Different audience members get different *kinds* of influence based on engagement, expertise, or willingness to pay:

- **Tier 0 (Free):** Watch + react. Your presence is felt (headcount, ambient energy)
- **Tier 1 (Ticket):** Participate in swarm decisions. Your pull has weight.
- **Tier 2 (Premium):** Propose options, not just choose between them. Submit ideas that get surfaced to the swarm.
- **Tier 3 (VIP):** Direct communication channel with performers. Your individual input is heard.

This solves the scalability/intimacy problem. Everyone participates, but at the level of investment they've chosen. The architecture ensures that 50K people don't produce chaos -- the system has natural filtering.

### 3. AI as Creative Mediator

An AI system sits between audience input and performer output, doing what no simple voting system can:

- **Signal extraction:** Parsing thousands of simultaneous inputs (text, emoji, audio, movement) into coherent creative direction. Not "52% voted for Option A" but "the crowd's energy is shifting from euphoric to melancholic, with a strong undercurrent of nostalgia."
- **Constraint generation:** Translating crowd sentiment into performer-friendly parameters. The AI doesn't tell the musician what to play -- it tells them "the room wants to go deeper and slower" and lets the artist interpret that.
- **Narrative coherence:** Ensuring that crowd-driven decisions produce a satisfying arc, not random walk. The AI has a model of dramatic structure and nudges the option space to maintain tension, build toward climax, and resolve.
- **Minority amplification:** Surfacing interesting minority preferences that the majority might not discover through pure democracy. "87% want more bass, but this 8% pocket wanting a cappella breakdown might be the more interesting choice" -- the AI can present it as an option.

### 4. The "Conducting" Metaphor

A new performer role: the **Crowd Conductor**. Like a symphony conductor, they don't play an instrument -- they shape the collective output of many participants. The Crowd Conductor reads the audience's inputs, interprets the AI-mediated signal, and makes real-time creative decisions that channel collective energy into coherent experience.

This is a new art form. The skill is reading crowds, feeling momentum, knowing when to follow the audience and when to redirect them. It combines DJ culture (reading the room), improv (yes-and), and conducting (shaping collective output).

### 5. Emotional Contagion Engine

Use biometric and behavioral signals (phone accelerometer for movement/dancing, microphone for crowd noise, camera for facial expression at opt-in) to create a real-time "emotional field" that remote and in-person audiences share. Remote viewers don't just see the crowd -- they *feel* the crowd's energy through haptic feedback, adaptive audio mixing, and visual representation.

The key insight: presence in a live audience is mostly about *emotional contagion* -- feeling what the people around you feel. If you can transmit that to remote viewers, you've solved 80% of the "being there" problem without VR headsets.

### 6. Forking Experiences

Instead of one show that everyone watches, a live event *branches* based on audience decisions. At decision points, the experience splits -- different audience segments get different continuations based on their choices. After diverging, branches can re-converge, and audiences compare what they experienced.

This requires multiple performance tracks running simultaneously (expensive for physical events, feasible for digital/hybrid). The "what did you get?" conversation afterward becomes the social hook -- like how Bandersnatch generated conversation, but live and shared.

### 7. Persistent Crowd Identity

Audiences that attend multiple events develop a collective identity and history. "This crowd chose the chaotic path last time -- do they do it again?" The system remembers past decisions and references them, creating continuity across events.

Over time, specific audience communities develop reputations and tendencies. This turns anonymous crowds into something like a character -- with a personality that emerges from thousands of individual choices accumulated over time.
